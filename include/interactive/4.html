Explore Norway's oily history

Norway has been drilling for oil since the late 60s, and is today one of the biggest oil and gas exporters in the world.

This has made Norway a very rich country over the last few decades, but it has also come at a cost: increasing greenhouse gas emissions, oil spills and a heavily oil dependant economy.

Explore the oily history of Norway, and hear from people who have been fighting against the powerful oil industry.

Norway's Biggest Emitter

After the 1980s, when the oil sector was growing rapidly, it quickly became the single most important contributor to Norway's emissions of greenhouse gases. Drag the slider below to see the historical emissions from the sector in Norway.

Exported Emission

The oil industry is without a doubt the largest source of greenhouse gases in Norway, but that hardly compares to all the emission it has exported to other countries in the form of oil and gas.

Annual, the exported emissions, which are created when oil and gas are burned in other countries, accounts for roughly 10 times all of Norways emissions.

Oil Spills

The oil industry is not only a big climate risk. Since the 60s, there has been numerous large oil spills in Norway. The largest was a blowout in 1977, but since then, there has been other large accidents with large amounts of oil spilling out in the ocean.

Common for them all, is that they where more than 70 km away from shore. Thankfully this prevented the oil from reaching the shores. But the potential oil resources outside of Lofoten, Vesterålen and Senja is much closer to land.

Beaches of Lofoten, Vesterålen and Senja

Lofoten, Vesterålen and Senja have some of the most stunning beaches and shore lines of Norway. They would be the first places to be hit, after a potential oil spill.
